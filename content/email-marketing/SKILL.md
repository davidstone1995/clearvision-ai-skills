# Email Marketing

**Requires:** clearvision-foundation

---

## What This Skill Does

Email is still the highest-ROI marketing channel for small businesses and organizations. The problem is most people write emails that read like newsletters nobody asked for, or promotions that feel like promotions. Good email marketing feels like a message from someone who knows you -- because it is.

This skill generates two types of email content:

1. **Clear Vision's own outreach emails** -- prospecting, nurture sequences, and follow-up to leads and past clients.
2. **Client campaign content** -- emails written for CV Pro Tools clients to send to their own lists (reactivation, birthday campaigns, seasonal promotions, review requests).

---

## What You Need to Provide

**Required:**
- Email type (see categories below)
- Audience: who is receiving this email (small business owner, church member, coaching client, nonprofit donor, etc.)
- Goal: what should happen after they read it (reply, book a call, click a link, leave a review, come back in, donate)

**For client campaign content:**
- Client name and business type
- Offer or message (what are they sending -- a promotion, a reactivation, a birthday message, a request)
- Any personalization fields available (first name, last name, last visit date, etc.)

## Email Categories

### 1. Clear Vision Prospecting and Nurture

For David's own outreach to prospects, leads, and past contacts. These are personal, direct, and short. They do not look like marketing emails.

**Types:**
- Cold outreach (first contact with a new prospect)
- Follow-up after a conversation or event
- Nurture sequence (3-5 emails over 2-4 weeks for a warm lead who hasn't said yes yet)
- Re-engagement (reaching back out to a past contact after a gap)

### 2. Client Campaign Content

Content written for a CV Pro Tools client to send to their own list. These should feel native to the client's voice and business -- not like a CV Pro Tools template.

**Types:**
- Reactivation campaign (reach lapsed customers)
- Birthday campaign
- Review request
- Seasonal promotion
- Welcome sequence (new customer or subscriber)
- Announcement (new service, new hours, event)

## Step 1: Identify the Email Type and Context

**Clear Vision prospecting emails:** Short. Personal. No "Dear Sir or Madam." No long pitch. David writes like he talks -- warm, direct, a specific reason for reaching out.

**Client campaign emails:** Calibrate to the client's audience and industry. A reactivation email for a pest control company sounds different from one for a church reaching lapsed attendees. Get the tone right for the audience before writing the email.

---

## Step 2: Generate the Email(s)

---

### CLEAR VISION PROSPECTING EMAIL

**Cold outreach (first contact):**

Subject lines that work: specific, not clever. Reference something real. Examples:
- "[Business Name] -- quick question"
- "Saw your Google reviews -- had a thought"
- "Something that might be worth 5 minutes"

Body structure:
- Line 1: Specific reason for reaching out. Not "I help businesses like yours." Something real. "I noticed [Business Name] doesn't have a way for customers to leave a review automatically -- that's something we fix in about a week."
- Lines 2-3: One sentence about what Clear Vision does, connected to that observation.
- Line 4: One low-friction ask. "Worth a quick call?" or "Would it be helpful if I sent over how it works?"
- Sign-off: First name. No title. No logo at the bottom of a cold email.

Length: Under 100 words. Every sentence earns its place.

---

**Follow-up after a conversation:**

Subject: "Following up -- [topic from the conversation]"

Body:
- Acknowledge the conversation in one sentence. Reference something specific from it.
- Deliver on anything promised (a link, more info, an answer to a question).
- Clear next step. Specific. One ask.

Length: Under 75 words.

---

**Nurture sequence (3-5 emails, sent over 2-4 weeks):**

Email 1 (Day 1): Value. No ask. Send something useful -- a short answer to a common question, a specific result from a similar business, one practical tip. The goal is to be worth opening.

Email 2 (Day 5-7): A story. An anonymized client result -- what was the problem, what changed, what happened. One paragraph. Brief.

Email 3 (Day 10-14): Address the most common objection. Not defensively -- just honestly. "I talk to a lot of business owners who say [objection]. Here's the honest answer: [response]."

Email 4 (Day 18-21): A gentle ask. "We've been in touch a few times now. If the timing is right, I'd love to get on a call. If not, no pressure -- I'll keep sending things I think are worth reading."

Email 5 (Day 28): Last touch. Light. "Just checking in one more time. If now isn't the right time, I completely understand. When it is, I'm here."

---

**Re-engagement (past contact, long gap):**

Subject: "Been a while -- checking in"

Body:
- Acknowledge the gap without apologizing for it.
- One sentence about something new or relevant.
- One low-pressure ask.

Length: Under 75 words. Should feel like a message from someone they know, not a reactivation campaign.

### CLIENT CAMPAIGN EMAIL TEMPLATES

---

**Reactivation Email (for clients sending to lapsed customers)**

Subject options:
- "We miss you at [Business Name]"
- "It's been a while -- we have something for you"
- "[First Name], we'd love to see you again"

Body:
> Hi [First Name],
>
> It's been [time period] since we last saw you at [Business Name]. We wanted to reach out and let you know we haven't forgotten about you.
>
> [Optional: One sentence about what's new -- new service, new offer, something that has changed since they last visited.]
>
> We'd love to have you back. [Offer -- be specific. A discount, a bonus, a free consultation. Something real, not vague.] Just [clear action -- reply, click, call] before [date if applicable].
>
> [Business Name]

Length: Under 100 words. Plain and personal. Not promotional in tone.

---

**Birthday Campaign Email**

Subject: "Happy Birthday, [First Name]!"

Body:
> Hi [First Name],
>
> Happy Birthday from all of us at [Business Name].
>
> To celebrate, we want to offer you [specific offer -- free service, discount, gift]. Just [action] before [expiration if applicable].
>
> Hope it's a great day.
>
> [Business Name]

Length: Under 60 words. Warm and brief. Nobody wants to read marketing copy on their birthday.

---

**Review Request Email**

Subject: "Quick favor -- how was your experience?"

Body:
> Hi [First Name],
>
> Thank you for choosing [Business Name]. We hope everything went well.
>
> If you have 60 seconds, we'd love it if you'd leave us a review on Google. Reviews help other people find us -- and they help us know how we're doing.
>
> [Review link]
>
> Thank you,
> [Business Name]

Length: Under 75 words. No pressure. No incentive (which violates Google's terms). Just a genuine ask.

---

**Welcome Email (new customer or subscriber)**

Subject: "Welcome to [Business Name] -- here's what to expect"

Body:
> Hi [First Name],
>
> Welcome -- and thank you for [action they took: joining our list, booking your first appointment, becoming a customer].
>
> [One sentence about what they can expect. Not a list of benefits. One honest sentence about what working with or following this business is like.]
>
> [If applicable: one specific next step -- book an appointment, follow on social, reply with a question.]
>
> We're glad you're here.
>
> [Business Name]

Length: Under 100 words. Warm without being effusive.

---

**Seasonal Promotion Email**

Subject: "[Season/occasion] offer from [Business Name]"

Body:
> Hi [First Name],
>
> [One sentence acknowledging the season or occasion -- without the cliché opening. Not "As the holidays approach..." Just: "The holidays are here." Or "Spring is the busiest time for [service type]."]
>
> [Offer -- what, how much, when it ends. Specific. Not vague.]
>
> [Clear action: "Call us," "Click here to book," "Reply to this email."]
>
> [Business Name]

Length: Under 100 words. Lead with the offer, not the setup.

---

## Output Quality Check

Before delivering any email:

- Subject line is specific -- not clever, not vague
- First line earns the second -- no preamble, no "I hope this email finds you well"
- One goal per email -- not multiple asks
- Plain language throughout -- no promotional vocabulary
- Length is appropriate: prospecting emails under 100 words, campaign emails under 150
- Every email ends with one clear, specific action
- Client campaign emails sound like the client, not like a template
- No em dashes
- No banned phrases from clearvision-foundation
- Nothing sounds like it was generated by a marketing platform
